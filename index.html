Scholarly Identity

A Scholar at the Crossroads
of Sport, Power & Culture

"Sport is not merely a physical activity — it is a site where power is reproduced, identities are contested, and geopolitical strategies are enacted."

Professor Mahfoud Amara is a transnational "bridge scholar" operating at the intersection of Algeria/North Africa, Western academia, and Qatar/the Gulf — translating concepts across Arabic, English, and French, and across disciplinary traditions, to contribute to the decolonisation and pluralisation of sport knowledge.

His work rejects reductionist and Orientalist frameworks. It foregrounds locally grounded interpretations of sport in Arab and Muslim societies, centres regional agency in debates too often dominated by Global North perspectives, and brings postcolonial and critical theory to bear on the very real governance structures shaping sport today.

Research Architecture

Five Core Research Themes

01

Sport, Power & Geopolitics

Foregrounding regional agency in debates about sport diplomacy, soft power, and mega-event hosting — moving beyond the reductive lens of "sportswashing" to examine how states in the Global South use sport as a strategic instrument.

02

Sport, Identity & Nation-building

Biographical and socio-historical approaches to how sport constructs national, ethnic, religious, and diasporic identities in Algeria, Qatar, and across the Arab world.

03

Media, Discourse & Counter-narratives

Critical discourse analysis (CDA) informed by Said's Orientalism and Foucauldian frameworks, examining how Western media constructs the Arab sporting subject and how counter-narratives emerge.

04

Sport Development, Ethics & Governance

Analysis of sport development policy in emerging nations, anti-doping governance through WADA and national agencies, and the political economy of international sport organisations.

05

Gender & Local Voices in MENA Sport

Critiquing homogenised Western narratives about gender in Arab sport; centring locally grounded epistemologies, women athletes' voices, and the complexity of change in culturally specific contexts.
